The '''1988 Stanley Cup Final''' was the [[Stanley Cup Final|championship series]] of the [[National Hockey League]]'s (NHL) [[1987–88 NHL season|1987–88 season]], and the culmination of the [[1988 Stanley Cup playoffs]]. It was contested between the [[1987–88 Edmonton Oilers season|Edmonton Oilers]] and [[1987–88 Boston Bruins season|Boston Bruins]]. The Oilers swept the Bruins to once again repeat as Stanley Cup champions. It was the Oilers’ fourth championship in franchise history.{{efn|name=game5}}

This was the seventh of nine consecutive Finals contested by a team from [[Western Canada]], sixth of eight by a team from [[Alberta]] (the Oilers appeared in six of them, the [[Calgary Flames]] in two, and the [[Vancouver Canucks]] in [[1982 Stanley Cup Final|one]]), and the last of five consecutive Finals to end with the Cup presentation on Alberta ice (the Oilers won four such Cups, the [[Montreal Canadiens]] the other). The series is remembered for the power failure that occurred during game four at [[Boston Garden]], which caused that game to be suspended. The league decided to replay game four at [[Northlands Coliseum]] in Edmonton, at the site, date and time that was originally scheduled for a possible game five. Game four is also the final time that [[Wayne Gretzky]] appeared in an Edmonton Oilers uniform as he was traded to [[Los Angeles Kings|Los Angeles]] just prior to the [[1988–89 NHL season|next season]], and the last Stanley Cup he would win as a player.

==Paths to the Finals==
{{further|1988 Stanley Cup playoffs}}
{{See also|1987–88 Boston Bruins season|1987–88 Edmonton Oilers season}}
The Oilers cruised into the Finals with relative ease, losing only two games in the process. They beat the [[Winnipeg Jets (1972-96)|Winnipeg Jets]] in five games, swept the [[Calgary Flames]], and then beat the [[Detroit Red Wings]] in five to win the [[Clarence S. Campbell Bowl]] for the fifth time in six years.

The Bruins, meanwhile, had a much harder road. It took them six games to knock off the [[Buffalo Sabres]], then beat their longtime rivals the [[Montreal Canadiens]] in five games, and then needed the full seven games to beat the [[New Jersey Devils]] to claim the [[Prince of Wales Trophy]].

==Game summaries==
The Finals pitted the Oilers' offensive juggernaut against the Bruins' more balanced team. The Oilers showed their defensive prowess, surrendering just nine goals in the four completed games. [[Ray Bourque]] was physical in defending against Gretzky, but that would not ground the "Great One" on his way to claiming his second [[Conn Smythe Trophy]] and setting playoff records with 31 assists in just 18 games, and 13 points in the Finals series.

===Game four (suspended)===
[[Glenn Anderson]] set a new record for quickest goal from the start of a Finals game when he scored ten seconds into the contest. That record was tied two years later in the third game of the {{scfy|1990}} Finals by [[John Byce]] who, in a twist, was playing for the Bruins ''against'' the Oilers.<ref>{{harvtxt|Diamond|2000|p=90}}</ref> Fog interfered with the game, requiring stoppages during the second period so that all 40+ players could skate around the ice to clear it away. The Oilers' [[Craig Simpson]] scored with 3:23 left in the second period, tying the game at 3–3, then the arena suffered a power failure before the ensuing face-off. The teams were sent to their [[dressing room]] until{{snd}}after a very long delay and no change in the situation{{snd}}[[NHL President]] [[John Ziegler Jr.]] announced that the game was suspended.<ref>{{cite book|last=Weekes|first=Don|title=The Best and Worst of Hockey's Firsts: The Unofficial Guide|year=2003|publisher=Greystone Books|location=Canada|isbn=9781550548600|pages=[https://archive.org/details/bestworstofhocke0000week/page/240 240]|url=https://archive.org/details/bestworstofhocke0000week/page/240}}</ref> Despite the game being suspended and replayed, Anderson's record is official.

Game four was subsequently rescheduled and moved to Edmonton, which was originally set to be the site of a game five if necessary. The Oilers won that game, sweeping the series and winning their fourth Stanley Cup in five years. The NHL announced that, in the event that the Bruins had managed to win game four, game five would have been played on the original date for game six in Boston, Edmonton would have hosted the rescheduled game six, and then game seven would have been played in Boston as the makeup game.<ref name=":0">{{cite news | url=https://www.latimes.com/archives/la-xpm-1988-05-25-sp-3235-story.html | title=Oilers, Bruins Play Game 4 in a Fog, End in the Dark | newspaper=Los Angeles Times | first=Jerry | last=Crowe | date=May 25, 1988}}</ref><ref name=":1">{{Cite news|last=Finn|first=Robin|date=1988-05-25|title=No Lights, Then No Crowd; Hockey Halted for the Night|language=en-US|work=The New York Times|url=https://www.nytimes.com/1988/05/25/sports/no-lights-then-no-crowd-hockey-halted-for-the-night.html|access-date=2021-08-01|issn=0362-4331}}</ref><ref name=":2">{{Citation|title=Memories: Power outage halts game at Boston Garden|url=https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=w07Zn4dtS3U |archive-url=https://ghostarchive.org/varchive/youtube/20211221/w07Zn4dtS3U |archive-date=2021-12-21 |url-status=live|language=en|access-date=2021-08-01}}{{cbignore}}</ref>

The 1988 Stanley Cup was presented to Oilers captain Wayne Gretzky by [[NHL Commissioner|NHL President]] [[John Ziegler Jr.|John Ziegler]] following the Oilers 6–3 win over the Bruins in game four.

;Team picture on the ice, after winning a championship
* After the Oilers won the 1988 Stanley Cup, Wayne Gretzky (in what ended up being his last game with the Oilers) requested a picture on the ice with all the players, and all non-playing members, including management, coaches, trainers, scouts, locker room assistants. The team honoured his request, and it has remained a tradition followed by each Stanley Cup-winning team. The team picture tradition after winning a championship then became a tradition followed by most hockey championship teams at all levels around the world.

Gretzky wanted every member of the Oilers to be included on the team picture. However, when the cup was engraved all five scouts were left off: [[Garnet Bailey]], [[Ed Chadwick]], [[Lorne Davis]], Matti Vaisanen (on the Cup in 1985, 1987, 1990), Bob Freeman (part time, not on the Cup). The Oilers also left three players off the Stanley Cup: Daryl Reaugh, Steve Dykstra, Jim Weimer. Bill Tuele (Public Relations Director) had his name added to the cup for the first time. He has rings with the Oilers in 1984, 1985, 1987, but his name was not put on the Stanley Cup those seasons.

==Broadcasting==
In the United States, this was the final year under [[ESPN National Hockey Night|ESPN]]'s national three-year deal. Under the U.S. TV contracts that would take effect beginning next season, [[NHL on SportsChannel America|SportsChannel America]] would take over as the NHL's American television partner.

ESPN's coverage of the 1988 Cup Finals was blacked out locally in the Boston area due to [[WSBK-TV|WSBK]] and [[NESN]]'s local rights to Bruins games.

In Canada, this was the second and final year that the English-language rights to the Cup Finals was split between the [[NHL on Global|Global-Canwest consortium]] and the [[Hockey Night in Canada|CBC]]. Global aired games one and two. The CBC aired game three, then both the original and replayed game fours. CBC had the rights to game 5 of the Stanley Cup Final, and Canwest/Global also had the rights to games 6 & 7 of the Stanley Cup Final between Edmonton Oilers and Boston Bruins (both CBC and Canwest/Global had the rights of Game 7, using separate production facilities and separate on-air talent), which were not necessary.<ref name=":0" /><ref name=":1" /><ref name=":2" />
