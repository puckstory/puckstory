The '''1986 Stanley Cup Final''' was the [[Stanley Cup Final|championship series]] of the [[National Hockey League]]'s (NHL) [[1985–86 NHL season|1985–86 season]], and the culmination of the [[1986 Stanley Cup playoffs]]. It was contested between the [[Western Conference (NHL)|Campbell Conference]] champion [[1985–86 Calgary Flames season|Calgary Flames]] and the [[Eastern Conference (NHL)|Wales Conference]] champion [[1985–86 Montreal Canadiens season|Montreal Canadiens]]. The Canadiens won the best-of-seven series in five games to win their 23rd [[Stanley Cup]], and their 17th in their last 18 Finals appearances dating back to {{scfy|1956}}.

It was the first all-Canadian Finals since Montreal lost to the [[Toronto Maple Leafs]] in {{scfy|1967}}, the last year of the [[Original Six]] era. This was the fifth of nine consecutive Finals contested by a team from [[Western Canada]], the fourth of eight contested by a team from [[Alberta]] (the [[Edmonton Oilers]] appeared in six, the Flames in two, the [[Vancouver Canucks]] in [[1982 Stanley Cup Final|one]]), and the third of five consecutive finals to end with the Cup presentation on Alberta ice (the Oilers won four, the Canadiens one). This was the only time between {{scfy|1980}} and {{scfy|1988}} that neither the Oilers (four wins) nor the [[New York Islanders]] (four wins) won the Stanley Cup.

Although this was the first ever postseason meeting between the two teams, it was not the first Montreal–Calgary Finals. The first Finals between teams from Montreal and Calgary took place in {{scfy|1924}} when the Canadiens defeated the [[Western Canada Hockey League]] champion [[Calgary Tigers]]. The Canadiens and Flames met again in a rematch in {{scfy|1989}}, with Calgary winning in six games.

The Finals reverted to the 2-2-1-1-1 format after implementing the 2-3-2 format in {{scfy|1984}}.<ref>{{cite news |url=https://www.newspapers.com/clip/49231310/1984-nhl-playoff-format-2-3-2/ |title=Playoff format changes made |date=September 23, 1983 |newspaper=The Ottawa Citizen|access-date=June 24, 2024|agency=United Press International |location=Ottawa, Ontario |page=40 |via=Newspapers.com}}</ref>

==Paths to the Finals==
{{see also|1986 Stanley Cup playoffs|1985–86 Calgary Flames season|1985–86 Montreal Canadiens season}}
Calgary defeated the [[Winnipeg Jets (1972–96)|Winnipeg Jets]] 3–0, the defending champion and [[Battle of Alberta|in-province rival]] [[Edmonton Oilers]] 4–3, and the [[St. Louis Blues]] 4–3 to advance to the final.

Montreal defeated [[Bruins–Canadiens rivalry|rival]] [[Boston Bruins]] 3–0, the [[Hartford Whalers]] 4–3, and the [[New York Rangers]] 4–1 to make it to the final.

===Game four===

{{NHLPlayoffs
|team1=Calgary Flames
|team2=Montreal Canadiens
|stadium1=[[Montreal Forum]]
|date1       =May 22
|score1      =0–1
|won1        =2

Game four remained scoreless until [[Claude Lemieux]] scored with 9:50 remaining in regulation, and [[Patrick Roy]] stopped all 15 shots he faced to record his first career playoff shutout, giving the Canadiens a 1–0 victory, and a commanding 3–1 series lead. The game ended in a bench-clearing brawl, during which Claude Lemieux bit the finger of [[Jim Peplinski]]. In response, Peplinski said following the incident, "I didn't know they allowed cannibalism in the NHL."<ref>{{cite web |last1=Fleming |first1=David |title=The tooth hurts: NHL's unsavory history of biting |url=https://www.espn.com/nhl/story/_/id/13013333/nhl-2015-stanley-cup-playoffs-andrew-shaw-carries-unsavory-biting-tradition-flem-file |website=ESPN.com |access-date=May 29, 2026 |date=June 4, 2015}}</ref> Both teams combined for a total of 176 penalty minutes; the most combined penalty minutes ever recorded in a Stanley Cup Final game.

===Game five===

{{NHLPlayoffs
|team1=Montreal Canadiens
|team2=Calgary Flames
|stadium1=[[Olympic Saddledome]]
|date1       =May 24
|score1      =4–3
|won1        =1

In game five, Montreal defeated Calgary at the Olympic Saddledome by a score of 4-3 to clinch the series 4-1, winning their 23rd Stanley Cup. Patrick Roy, who made 30 saves in the victory, was awarded the [[Conn Smythe Trophy]] as playoffs MVP. At just 20 years old, Roy became the youngest player in NHL history to receive the award.

==Riot==
Some 5,000 jubilant Montreal fans celebrating the Canadiens' Stanley Cup win over the Calgary Flames rampaged through the city's downtown, causing over {{CAD|1 million}} worth of damage.<ref>[https://nationalpost.com/Riots+black+Montreal/464332/story.html nationalpost.com]{{dead link|date=October 2025|bot=medic}}{{cbignore|bot=medic}} </ref>

==Broadcasting==
In Canada, this was the second and final year that the English-language rights of the Cup Finals were shared between [[Hockey Night in Canada|CBC]] and [[NHL on CTV|CTV]]. For games one and two, CBC only had the rights to air them locally in Montreal and Calgary, while CTV broadcast them to the rest of the country. CBC then had the exclusive rights to televise games three, four, and five nationally. Had the series gone to a seventh game, then both CBC and CTV would have simultaneously televised it while using their separate production facilities and crews. After the season, CTV pulled the plug on their two-year-long venture with the NHL, and their rights package was eventually given to the [[NHL on Global|Global-Canwest consortium]].

This was the first of three consecutive seasons that [[ESPN National Hockey Night|ESPN]] televised the Stanley Cup Final in the United States.
