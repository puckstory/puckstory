The '''1979 Stanley Cup Final''' was the [[Stanley Cup Final|championship series]] of the [[National Hockey League]]'s (NHL) [[1978–79 NHL season|1978–79 season]], and the culmination of the [[1979 Stanley Cup playoffs]]. The [[1978–79 New York Rangers season|New York Rangers]] challenged the defending champion [[1978–79 Montreal Canadiens season|Montreal Canadiens]], who made their fourth straight appearance. It was New York's first foray into the Final since {{scfy|1972}}. The Canadiens would win the best-of-seven series, four games to one, to win their fourth consecutive [[Stanley Cup]] championship.<ref>{{cite web|url= https://news.google.com/newspapers?nid=feST4K8J0scC&dat=19790522&printsec=frontpage&hl=en|title= Canadiens do it again|date=May 22, 1979|work=St Petersburg Times (Page 21)|access-date=June 12, 2013 }}</ref>

This was the first of six consecutive Finals involving a team from the [[New York metropolitan area]]. The next five Finals would be contested by the Rangers' crosstown rivals the [[New York Islanders]], who would win the first four of those series to forge a dynasty matching that of the Canadiens. By defeating the Rangers, the Canadiens completed the rare accomplishment of winning four consecutive titles in a North American league competition consisting of at least sixteen teams, and remain the only team based outside the New York metropolitan area to do so. Prior to the Canadiens' dynasty, the feat had been achieved only twice before, both times by the [[New York Yankees]] in [[Major League Baseball]]. The aforementioned Islanders are the only team to accomplish it since. This was also the last Stanley Cup Final until [[2013 Stanley Cup Final|2013]] where both teams were from the [[Original Six]]. An Original Six club would not reach the Final again until Montreal won their next championship in [[1986 Stanley Cup Final|1986]].

==Paths to the Final==
{{further|1978–79 NHL season|1978–79 Montreal Canadiens season|1978–79 New York Rangers season}}
Montreal defeated the [[Toronto Maple Leafs]] 4–0 and the [[Boston Bruins]] 4–3 (highlighted by the "too many men on the ice" game seven overtime win) to advance to the Final.

New York defeated the [[Los Angeles Kings]] 2–0, the [[Philadelphia Flyers]] 4–1 and the [[New York Islanders]] 4–2 to make it to the Final.

==Game summaries==
The Canadiens won the Cup in five games, winning it on home ice for the first time since {{scfy|1968}}.<ref>{{cite book|title=The Montreal Canadiens:100 Years of Glory|first=D'Arcy|last=Jenish|page=234|publisher=Doubleday|year=2009|isbn=978-0-385-66325-0}}</ref> After the game [[Jacques Lemaire]], [[Yvan Cournoyer]], and [[Ken Dryden]] retired, while head coach [[Scotty Bowman]] left the Canadiens to join the [[Buffalo Sabres]], which would mark the end of the Canadiens' dynasty. Montreal Canadiens scored 46 total points during the Stanley Cup Final, while the New York Rangers scored 26 points in the Final.<ref>{{Cite web|title=1979 NHL Stanley Cup Final: MTL vs. NYR|url=https://www.hockey-reference.com/playoffs/1979-montreal-canadiens-vs-new-york-rangers-stanley-cup-final.html|access-date=2021-08-16|website=Hockey-Reference.com|language=en}}</ref>

This Final marked the second time in four years that Bowman and [[Fred Shero]] coached against each other. In {{scfy|1976}}, they coached against each other, though Shero was with the [[Philadelphia Flyers]].

==Broadcasting==
The Stanley Cup Final were produced by [[Hockey Night in Canada|CBC]], who carried the game in Canada and were shown in the United States on the [[The NHL Network (1975–79)|NHL's syndicated package]]. [[Dan Kelly (sportscaster)|Dan Kelly]] called the play-by-play for Games 1, 3, 4, and 5 entirely and split game 2 with [[Danny Gallivan]]. [[Gary Dornhoefer]] served as color commentator for Games 1 and 5, [[Gerry Pinder]] served as color commentator for Game 2 only, [[Bobby Orr]] served as color commentator from Madison Square Garden. Meanwhile, [[Dick Irvin Jr.]] served as color commentator for the entire Final series and hosted the games in Montreal, [[Dave Hodge]] and [[Howie Meeker]] hosted the games in New York City. [[NHL on ABC|ABC]] was contracted to televise game seven.<ref>{{cite news|date=May 13, 1979|title=NHL, ABC-TV Agree|page=89|newspaper=Reading Eagle|agency=Associated Press}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|date=1979-05-13|title=May 26 Selected For a 7th Game|work=[[The New York Times]]|url=https://www.nytimes.com/1979/05/13/archives/may-26-selected-for-a-7th-game.html|access-date=February 9, 2016}}</ref> Since the series ended in five games, the contract was void.<ref>{{cite news|title=Montreal win kills ABC TV deal but Ziegler feels pact is on way|last=Ramsay|first=Donald|newspaper=The Globe and Mail|date=May 22, 1979|page=35}}</ref>
