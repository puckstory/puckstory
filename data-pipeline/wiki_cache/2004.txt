The '''2004 Stanley Cup Final''' was the [[Stanley Cup Final|championship series]] of the [[National Hockey League]]'s (NHL) [[2003–04 NHL season|2003–04 season]], and the culmination of the [[2004 Stanley Cup playoffs]]. The [[Eastern Conference (NHL)|Eastern Conference]] champion [[2003–04 Tampa Bay Lightning season|Tampa Bay Lightning]] defeated the [[Western Conference (NHL)|Western Conference]] champion [[2003–04 Calgary Flames season|Calgary Flames]] in seven games for their first Stanley Cup in franchise history, becoming the southernmost team to win the [[Stanley Cup]] until their in-state rival [[Florida Panthers]] won the Cup in {{scfy|2024}}. It was Tampa Bay's first-ever appearance in the Finals in their twelfth season since entering the league in [[1992–93 NHL season|1992]]. For Calgary, it was the team's third appearance, and first since their championship season of {{scfy|1989}}. This was one of the few Stanley Cup Finals in which the losing team scored more goals.

Lightning owner [[Bill Davidson (businessman)|William Davidson]] became the first owner in sports history to win two championships in one year as eight days after this series ended, the other team that Davidson owned (the [[2003–04 Detroit Pistons season|Detroit Pistons]] of the [[National Basketball Association|NBA]]) won the [[2004 NBA Finals|NBA title]] in five games over the [[2003–04 Los Angeles Lakers season|Los Angeles Lakers]]. This was the last Stanley Cup Final to be played for two years, as the [[2004–05 NHL lockout]] began three months after the end of this series, lasting over ten months and leading to the cancellation of the following season, with the league not returning to play for the Stanley Cup until [[2006 Stanley Cup Final|2006]]. This was the last of three consecutive Finals to feature a team making its debut appearance.

===Tampa Bay Lightning===
Tampa Bay finished the season with 106 points and entered the playoffs as the [[Eastern Conference (NHL)|Eastern Conference’s]] top seed.<ref>{{Cite web |title=Tampa Bay Lightning Franchise History |url=https://www.hockey-reference.com/teams/TBL/history.html |access-date=2022-05-02 |website=Hockey-Reference.com |language=en}}</ref> They defeated the eighth, seventh, and third-seeded teams, beating the [[2003–04 New York Islanders season|New York Islanders]] 4–1, the [[2003–04 Montreal Canadiens season|Montreal Canadiens]] 4–0 and the [[2003–04 Philadelphia Flyers season|Philadelphia Flyers]] 4–3, in order, and they advanced to the Finals for the first time in franchise history since their establishment in [[1992–93 Tampa Bay Lightning season|1992]].<ref>{{Cite web |title=2003-04 Tampa Bay Lightning Roster and Statistics |url=https://www.hockey-reference.com/teams/TBL/2004.html |access-date=May 2, 2022 |website=Hockey-Reference.com |language=en}}</ref> It was also the third year in a row in which a team made their debut Finals appearance, after the [[2002 Stanley Cup Final|Carolina Hurricanes]] and [[2003 Stanley Cup Final|Mighty Ducks of Anaheim]].

===Calgary Flames===
Calgary finished the season with 94 points, qualifying for the playoffs for the first time since [[1995–96 Calgary Flames season|1996]].<ref>{{Cite web |title=Calgary Flames Franchise History |url=https://www.hockey-reference.com/teams/CGY/history.html |access-date=2022-05-02 |website=Hockey-Reference.com |language=en}}</ref> As the sixth seed, they defeated the [[Western Conference (NHL)|Western Conference]]'s top three seeded teams, which were the [[2003–04 Vancouver Canucks season|Vancouver Canucks]] 4–3, the [[2003–04 Detroit Red Wings season|Detroit Red Wings]] 4–2 and the [[2003–04 San Jose Sharks season|San Jose Sharks]] 4–2, in order, and made it to the Finals for the first time since [[1988–89 Calgary Flames season|1989]].<ref>{{Cite web |title=2003-04 Calgary Flames Roster and Statistics |url=https://www.hockey-reference.com/teams/CGY/2004.html |access-date=May 2, 2022 |website=Hockey-Reference.com |language=en}}</ref> This also marked the first time a [[Hockey in Canada|Canadian]] team made it to the Finals since the [[Vancouver Canucks]] lost to the [[1993–94 New York Rangers season|New York Rangers]] in the [[1994 Stanley Cup Final]].<ref name="CanadianTeams">{{cite web|quote=Calgary is the first Canadian team to reach the Stanley Cup finals since the 1994 Vancouver Canucks...lost...to the New York Rangers.|url=https://www.cbc.ca/sports/flames-reach-stanley-cup-finals-1.514689|title=Flames reach Stanley Cup finals|date=May 20, 2004|access-date=May 28, 2023|work=CBC Sports|publisher=[[Canadian Broadcasting Corporation]]}}</ref>

The first game, at [[St. Pete Times Forum]], saw the Flames win 4–1. Dave Andreychuk began the game with a record 634 career goals without a Stanley Cup Final appearance.<ref>{{cite web|title=NHL playoffs 2018: Alex Ovechkin, Capitals join Golden Knights in unlikely Stanley Cup Final|url=https://www.sportingnews.com/us/nhl/news/nhl-playoffs-2018-capitals-lightning-game-7-live-score-result-updates-video-highlights/1ajd713bp7rbk12werl14hfg2l|website=The Sporting News|access-date=28 May 2023}}</ref> Calgary only got 19 shots off against the Lightning defence, but more than one-fifth found the net. [[Martin Gélinas|Martin Gelinas]] got Calgary on the board early, and they extended the lead to 3–0 in the second period on goals by [[Jarome Iginla]], his 11th of the playoffs, and [[Stéphane Yelle|Stephane Yelle]]. [[Chris Simon]] added the fourth and final Calgary goal after Tampa Bay's [[Martin St. Louis]] scored the lone Lightning goal.

Game 2 saw the same final score, but this time, it was Tampa Bay winning a clutch game to tie the series, 1–1, headed to Calgary. [[Ruslan Fedotenko]]'s 10th goal of the postseason got the Lightning on the board first, and Tampa Bay used three third-period goals, coming from [[Brad Richards]], [[Dan Boyle (ice hockey)|Dan Boyle]], and St. Louis, respectively, to blast the game open. The lone Calgary goal was scored by [[Ville Nieminen]].

These Finals would be the last until [[2013 Stanley Cup Final|2013]] to be tied after two games. The team with home ice in games one and two held a 2–0 edge in every Final between 2006 and 2011. In 2012, the [[Los Angeles Kings]] won the first two games at [[New Jersey Devils|New Jersey]].

With a chance to take a commanding 3–1 series lead, Calgary was shut out by Lightning goalie [[Nikolai Khabibulin]], who recorded his fifth shutout of the postseason, a 29-save shutout, in a 1–0 Tampa Bay victory, with the game's lone goal being scored by Brad Richards three minutes into the game on a two-man advantage.

With 4:13 left in the game, Ville Nieminen checked [[Vincent Lecavalier]] into the boards from behind, drawing a five-minute major penalty for boarding, a game misconduct penalty, and an eventual game five suspension.<ref>{{cite web | url=https://www.espn.com/nhl/recap?gameId=240531003 | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20180208174750/http://www.espn.com/nhl/recap?gameId=240531003 | url-status=dead | archive-date=February 8, 2018 | title=Richards nets record seventh winning goal | publisher=ESPN | date=May 31, 2004 | access-date=July 6, 2009}}</ref> Meanwhile, fans at the [[Pengrowth Saddledome]] angrily booed referees Kerry Fraser and Brad Watson throughout most of the contest. They were originally also scheduled to work game six in Calgary but the league eventually decided to replace them.<ref>{{cite web | url=http://sportsillustrated.cnn.com/2004/writers/stephen_cannella/06/05/cannella.preview/index.html | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20040619000645/http://sportsillustrated.cnn.com/2004/writers/stephen_cannella/06/05/cannella.preview/index.html | url-status=dead | archive-date=June 19, 2004 | title=A faint whiff of panic: Lightning tightly wound with Flames nearing first title in 15 years | publisher=SI.com | date=June 4, 2004 | access-date=July 6, 2009}}</ref><ref>{{cite web | url=https://www.nytimes.com/2004/06/07/sports/hockey-the-gamesmanship-is-over-it-s-one-game-for-the-cup.html?pagewanted=print | title= The Gamesmanship Is Over: It's One Game for the Cup | work=New York Times | date=June 7, 2004 | access-date=July 6, 2009|first=Joe|last=Lapointe}}</ref>

The series returned to Tampa Bay tied, 2–2, for a critical game five, and Calgary pulled off a 3–2 overtime victory to move within one win from the Stanley Cup. After Gelinas and St. Louis traded goals in the first period, Iginla scored for Calgary late in the second period. However, [[Fredrik Modin]] tied the game for the Lightning 37 seconds into the third period. The 2–2 score held until after 14:40 had gone by in overtime, when [[Oleg Saprykin]]'s first goal since the first round won the game for the Flames.

===Game six===
{{NHLPlayoffs

Back to Calgary for game six, each team scored two second-period goals, with Richards scoring two for the Lightning and [[Chris Clark (ice hockey)|Chris Clark]] and [[Marcus Nilson]] for the Flames. In the third period, there was a dispute over a Martin Gelinas redirect that appeared to have gone in off of his skate.<ref>{{YouTube|id=qBBgCRIxrtg|title=Martin Gelinas Phantom Goal, Did The Puck Go In?}}</ref> A review from one camera angle appeared to show the puck crossing the goal line before Khabibulin's pad dragged it out, though some (including Lightning [[Tim Taylor (ice hockey, born 1969)|Tim Taylor]]) argue that the puck had not only been knocked several inches ''above'' the goal line (thus making there appear to be white ice between the puck and the goal line) in front of Khabibulin's pad, but that it was also "kicked" by Gelinas. The play was never reviewed. However, the ABC broadcast of Game 7 showed a CGI video analysis of the play, which estimated that the puck did not completely cross the line, and that the call on the ice was correct.<ref>{{YouTube|id=qLav481BVEA|title=A better look at Martin Gelinas 'goal' in game six Stanley Cup Final 2004}}</ref> The CGI company that did the analysis of the video was based out of Calgary. The game entered overtime with the Flames needing only a goal to win the Stanley Cup. However, thirty-three seconds into the second overtime, St. Louis put in the game-winner for the Lightning to force a winner-take-all seventh game in Tampa.

===Game seven===
{{NHLPlayoffs

In a tense Game 7, Fedotenko scored goals for Tampa Bay late in the first period and late in the second period for a 2–0 lead. After [[Craig Conroy|Conroy]] scored to narrow the deficit to 2–1, Calgary bombarded Khabibulin after taking only seven shots in the first two periods. After the Conroy goal, Khabibulin stopped 16 Calgary shots. The series ended as Flames center [[Marcus Nilson]] missed a last-second opportunity to force overtime. Tampa Bay won the game, 2–1, and the Stanley Cup.

===Engraving notes===
* #9 [[Éric Perrin|Eric Perrin]] (C) played in 4 regular season games and 12 playoff games (4 in the Eastern Conference finals). #38 [[Darren Rumble]] (D) played in 5 regular season games and did not play in the playoffs (he was a healthy scratch). #2 [[Stanislav Neckář|Stanislav Neckar]] (D) played in 2 games in the Eastern Conference finals. Neckar was on the [[Nashville Predators]] injured reserve list for most of the season, before joining Tampa Bay in a trade on March 9, 2004. As they did not automatically qualify,{{efn|name=qualify}} Tampa Bay successfully requested an exemption to engrave their names.
* [[Ruslan Fedotenko]] was the first player who was born and raised in Ukraine, and exclusively trained in the country to win the Stanley Cup.
*All 52 members were included with their full first and last names on the presentation Stanley Cup, filling the last spot on it. When the engraver Louise St. Jacques went to engrave the replica Stanley Cup, there was less space available. There was more space between each winning team on the replica Stanley Cup than on the presentation Stanley Cup. Louise decided to keep each member's name in the same order on the same line on the replica Stanley Cup, so all names were engraved with their first initial and full last name. This is another way of telling the presentation Stanley Cup from the replica Stanley Cup. (see [[1984 Stanley Cup Final]] and [[1993 Stanley Cup Final]])
* #24 [[Shane Willis]] (RW – 12 regular season games) and #40 [[Brian Eklund]] (G – 0 regular season games, 62 for [[Pensacola Ice Pilots|Pensacola]] of the [[ECHL]]) did not play in or dress for the playoffs (both were healthy scratches). Both were on the roster during the Final, but left off the Stanley Cup engraving due to not qualifying.{{efn|name=qualify}} They were not in the team picture.<ref>[https://www.tampabay.com/archive/2004/08/21/willis-re-signs-with-lightning/ Willis re-signs with Lightning]<br/>[https://www.patriotledger.com/story/sports/nhl/2020/11/25/abingtons-eklund-moving-up-coaching-ranks/6420599002/ Braintree native Brian Eklund making the move back to pro hockey]</ref>

==Broadcasting==
In the United States, this was the last Stanley Cup Final to air on ABC and the ESPN family of networks until the [[2022 Stanley Cup Final|2022 Finals]]. ESPN televised the first two games while ABC broadcast the rest of the series. Due to the [[2004–05 NHL lockout]], which suspended play for the next season, this marked the end of ESPN's third run and ABC's second run as the main NHL broadcasters. [[NHL on NBC|NBC]] and [[NHL on Versus|OLN]] would pick up the rights to broadcast the NHL for the {{NHL Year|2005}} season. The [[Comcast]]-owned OLN would later be renamed Versus for the {{NHL Year|2006}} season, then re-branded as [[NBCSN]] on January 2, 2012, following Comcast's 2011 acquisition of NBC, effectively moving to the ''NHL on NBC'' banner. The Finals would also be the last time a Canadian team played a game on ABC (regular season and postseason) until 2024.

In Canada, the CBC's broadcast of game seven of the Finals drew 4.862 million viewers, making it the highest-rated NHL game on the CBC since game seven of the 1994 Final, which drew 4.957 million viewers.<ref name="CBC"/> However, those numbers include both pre-game and post-game coverage. The game itself drew 5.560 million viewers, up from 5.404 in 1994.<ref name="CBC">{{cite news|title=game seven scores with Canadian viewers|date=June 9, 2004|newspaper=The Calgary Herald|page=AA.05}}</ref>
